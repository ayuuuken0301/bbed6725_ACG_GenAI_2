Higher Production Speed and Cost Reduction

The Current Crisis in the Industry

The anime industry is facing a "scissors effect." While the market has been growing, the domestic labor supply of animators in Japan is shrinking. Declining birth rate and brain drain caused by poor compensation led to talent shortage in the industry.

The Existing Bottleneck

In traditional anime production, there are many low-value-added tasks that are labour-intensive, such as:

GenAI to Facilitate Labour Force Reallocation

By utilizing GenAI to automate these non-critical tasks, labor costs for completing them can be reduced because less time and labor are needed. Consequently, manpower can be shifted to essential key tasks instead.

Potential Risks

Risk 1: Can AI Reflect the Intrinsic Meaning of Expression?

GenAI art offers immediate, cost-effective solutions for mass production, maximizing business output. However, can AI reflect the creator's "intrinsic meaning," intent, or original expression?

Hayao Miyazaki has openly rejected using AI-generated art in his work, calling AI-type images an insult to life and saying he would never incorporate such technology into Studio Ghibli projects, which implicitly defends hand-drawn, human-made animation as the studio's core.

AI-generated images cannot replace human-made art, why?

A research paper published in the US explored this question. Researchers found that even when images are visually identical, customers consistently place a higher value on work created by human hands.


Participants were shown artworks and randomly given either a "Human-created" or "AI-generated" label. Even though the image was actually made by AI, the human-labeled images were rated significantly higher in terms of Like, Beauty, emotional depth, and Monetary Worth.

Note: Interpreting each factor

  • Monetary Worth: The perceived financial value and how much someone is willing to pay for the work.
  • Emotional Depth: The perceived soul or deep meaning behind the image.

Effort Heuristic

The study found that a major driver of value is the perceived effort and narrative behind a piece. Customers don't just buy an image; they buy the story, time, effort, and the intentionality that only a human creator can provide. When people know a human spent time and care on a project, they find the result more profound and are willing to invest more in it.

Risk 2: Operational Risks of Integrating GenAI into Anime Production

While GenAI is exceptionally powerful in terms of speed, the successful implementation of these tools into a production workflow still requires significant human effort. A studio cannot simply "plug in" GenAI by subscribing to or developing a tool. It requires a fundamental change in the creative mindset and operational structure.


There are three challenges that studios may face when integrating GenAI into their production processes:

1. The Knowledge Gap of Digital Literacy

‚ñº

Traditional animators are trained in physical or digital brushwork, not AI skills nor "Prompt Engineering". Without formal upskilling, employees may struggle to communicate their artistic intent to the AI. This often leads to a Trial and Error Loop, where refining AI outputs takes more time than manual drawing, defeating the purpose of the technology.

2. The Ceiling of Creativity

‚ñº

Current GenAI models excel at common assets, such as generic school corridors or standard cityscapes. However, the ACG industry thrives on unique world-building. For projects featuring uncommon backgrounds or avant-garde settings, AI often falls into a Generalization Trap. This results in work that feels "AI-ish", it looks perfect but lacking the "soul" and nuance required for high-end production.

3. Quality Control

‚ñº

The inherent design of GenAI makes it need to rebuilds each frame or asset from scratch, even those they look the same by us. This might leads to inconsistency on character features, especially on some tiny stuff, such as the number of buttons on a jacket.

Mitigation Measures

To navigate the integration of GenAI while maintaining the uniqueness of artwork and its economic value, the animation company can consider taking the following strategies:

1. Workforce Upskilling

To bridge the "Knowledge Gap" of using digital tools, animators need transform their role from manual labour to a creative director of AI tools.


Training includes:

AI Literacy

‚ñº

Provide training for employees on how to use GenAI tools to translate their artistic intent accurately.

Mindset Development

‚ñº

Educate staff on the strategic use of GenAI to ensure they do not over-rely on digital tools, thereby preserving their unique creativity and foundational drawing skills.

2. Transparency on AI Usage

To combat the "Effort Heuristic" risk‚Äîwhere consumers value human work more‚Äîstudios must be transparent about the production process.

Disclosure on Production Process

‚ñº

Indicate which segments (e.g., static backgrounds) used AI assistance and which (e.g., key character animation) were entirely human-made through disclosures on official websites or annual report

Behind-the-Scenes Booklets

‚ñº

Sell "Production Art Books" that detail the human decision-making process. By showing that AI was a tool guided by human directors, studios can maintain the "Premium Value" and emotional depth audiences expect.

3. Developing Proprietary Model

To solve the "Ceiling of Creativity", studios can develop in-house tailor-made AI models, instead of using the public and generic AI models.

In-House Training Data

‚ñº

Develop proprietary GenAI models trained exclusively on the studio's own historical library of graphics and character designs.
